## Teaching the listener what he/she doesn't know: ~거든(요)

~거든(요) is used to provide **new information** to the listener. This is the **opposite** of ~잖아(요).

### Examples

- **문을 닫았어요. 이제 냉방 중이거든요.** = I closed the door. Because the air conditioner is running.
- **셔츠를 입어야 될 것 같아요. 이두근이 너무 크거든요.** = Looks like I'd better put on a shirt. Because my biceps are too big.
- **컴퓨터를 쓸 때 고개를 그렇게 숙이지 마. 목이 아프거든** = Don't bend your head forward when using a computer. Your neck will be sore.

### Past Tense

- **물속에 안 들어갔어. 너무 무서웠거든.** = I didn't go into the water. Because it was too scary.

### Future Tense

- **담배를 피워 보지 마세요. 한번만 해 보면 바로 중독될 것이거든요** = Don't try smoking. If you try it once, you'll get addicted.

---

## Setting up an upcoming sentence

~거든(요) often sets up for information that makes sense because the listener heard the new information:

- **제가 캐나다 사람이거든요. 그래서 프랑스어도 할 수 있어요** = I'm Canadian. So, I can speak French as well.

---

## ~거든 as "if"

Used between clauses similar to ~(으)면, usually with imperative endings:

- **바지가 너무 크거든 교환해!** = If the pants are too big, change them!

---

## 아니거든! (Disagreement)

- **그 남자가 너무 잘생겼어 / 아니거든!** = That man is so handsome / No! (He's not!)
