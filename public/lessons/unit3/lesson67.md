## Introduction

In this lesson, you will learn the meanings of **~처럼** and **~듯(이)**. These are often used to indicate that an action is done "like" something else.

---

## Like (a noun): (명사)처럼

When **~처럼** is attached to a noun, it often indicates that an action is done "like" the way the specified noun does it.

**Examples:**
- 돼지처럼 = like a pig
- 아빠가 돼지처럼 먹고 있어요 = Dad is eating like a pig

**Common constructions:**
- 나처럼 = like me
- 예전처럼 = like old times
- 평소처럼 = like usual
- 원숭이처럼 = like a monkey
- 그 전처럼 = like before

**Example sentences:**
- 저는 그 전처럼 하고 싶어요 = I want to do it like before
- 재료를 비빔밥처럼 비벼야 돼요 = Mix the ingredients like 비빔밥
- 바보처럼 왜 이렇게 상식이 없어요? = Why, like an idiot, do you not have any common sense?

> **Note:** If you are indicating that something happens in a place, and you want to describe the similarities with that action happening in another place, **~처럼** can be attached to **~에서**.
> - 유럽에서처럼 매일 아침에 빵을 먹고 싶어요 = Like in Europe, I want to eat bread every morning

> **Note:** In Lesson 36 you learned how to say a person looks like something else. You can use **~처럼** to describe that a particular scenario "looks like" something:
> - 네가 넘어질 것처럼 보였어 = It looked like you were going to fall

## As if…: ~듯(이)

By describing **~듯(이)** with a clause using **~는 것**, you can create the meaning of "as if…"

**Examples:**
- 그를 알아보지 못하는 듯이… = As if he didn't recognize him…
- 그가 이해하지 못하는 듯이… = As if he didn't understand…
- 그는 떠나기 싫다는 듯이… = As if he didn't want to leave…
- 그가 죽은 듯이… = As if he were dead

**Example sentences:**
- 그는 떠나기 싫다는 듯이 울었어요 = He cried as if he didn't want to leave
- 그는 이해하지 못하는 듯이 행동했어요 = He acted as if he didn't understand
- 그는 죽은 듯이 바닥에 누워 있었어요 = He lay on the ground as if he were dead
- 마감일까지 숙제를 다 하려고 미친 듯이 공부를 했다 = In order to finish all of my homework by the deadline, he studied as if he was crazy
- 그 사람이 무기를 소지한 듯이 움직이기 시작했어요 = That person started moving as if he possessed a weapon

> **Note:** ~듯이 can also be placed directly to the stem of a verb/adjective. There is a difference between ~하는 듯이 and ~하듯이:
> - When **듯이 is not attached directly to the stem**, the nuance is that the clause before 듯이 is somewhat of a guess.
> - When **~듯이 is added directly to a stem**, one is indicating that the two clauses are exactly the same (no guessing involved). The clause before ~듯이 is usually some ridiculous metaphor.

**Examples of direct attachment (metaphorical):**
- 그는 물을 쓰듯이 돈을 썼다 = He used money as if it was water
- 말이 날뛰듯이 그의 심장이 뛰었다 = His heart was racing, as if a horse were bucking
- 폭포가 떨어지듯이 그는 눈물을 흘렸다 = He cried as if it was a waterfall

> **Note:** The word "듯하다" can be placed at the end of a sentence to mean "it seems like."
> - 선생님은 이것을 잘 모르는 듯해요 = The teacher seems as if he doesn't understand this

## 마치

The word **"마치"** is used in sentences to give the listener the expectation or feeling that one situation will be likened to another. It is common to see 마치 used in sentences with **~처럼** or **~듯이**.

**Examples:**
- 마치 말처럼 달렸어요 = He ran like a horse
- 마치 그는 죽은 듯이 바닥에 누워 있었어요 = He lay on the floor as if he were dead
