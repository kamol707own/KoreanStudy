### Introduction

Most of what you learned in Unit 1 was taught simply to be a foundation to what you will learn in Unit 2. When learning Korean, you eventually reach a point where the only thing you are learning is other ways to end sentences. There are hundreds upon hundreds of different things that you can add to the end of sentences to give a sentence a different meaning.

Today you will learn the most important aspect of Korean grammar: **~는 것**. Understanding this concept will literally make your ability to create sentences increase dramatically.

---

### ~는 것: Modifying Nouns with Verbs

Adjectives get placed before nouns to describe them. You learned this in Lesson 4. For example:
- **예쁘다** = **예쁜** (pretty)
- **똑똑하다** = **똑똑한** (smart)
- **젊다** = **젊은** (young)

To describe nouns:
- **예쁜 여자** = Pretty girl
- **똑똑한 학생** = Smart student
- **젊은 선생님** = Young teacher

But verbs can also describe nouns in this same way. In English, when a verb describes a noun, the verb comes after the noun:
- The girl who walks
- The student who studies
- The teacher who eats

In Korean, verbs are placed before nouns to describe them - very similar to how it is done with adjectives.

When you adjust verbs to describe nouns, you must add **~는** to the stem of a verb:
- **걷다** = **걷는**
- **공부하다** = **공부하는**
- **먹다** = **먹는**

These can be placed before a noun to describe it:
- **걷는 여자** = the girl who walks
- **공부하는 학생** = the student who studies
- **먹는 선생님** = the teacher who eats

Once you have made a noun that is being described by a verb, you can place that noun in sentences that end in an adjective, verb, or **이다**.

Examples:
- **제가 만나고 있는 사람은 예뻐요** = The person I am meeting is pretty
- **제가 보고 있는 영화는 재미있어요** = The movie I am watching is funny
- **제가 먹고 있는 음식은 맛있어요** = The food I am eating is delicious
- **저는 제가 자주 가는 곳에 가고 있어요** = I am going to the place I often go to

---

### ~는 것 Past Tense (~ㄴ/은 것)

You can also describe nouns with verbs in the past tense. Instead of adding **~는** to the stem, you add **~ㄴ/은**:
- Add **~은** to stems ending in a consonant (**먹다** = **먹은**)
- Add **~ㄴ** directly to stems ending in a vowel (**가다** = **간**)

Examples:
- **제가 간 곳** = The place I went
- **제가 먹은 음식** = The food I ate
- **제가 가르친 학생** = The student I taught

More examples:
- **엄마가 요리한 음식은 너무 맛있어요** = The food my mom cooked is delicious
- **제가 어제 만난 사람은 저를 다시 만나고 싶어요** = The person I met yesterday wants to meet me again
- **남은 음식을 포장하고 싶어요** = I want to pack up the food that is left over

---

### ~는 것 Future Tense (~ㄹ/을 것)

You can also describe nouns with verbs in the future tense. Instead of adding **~는** to the stem, you add **~ㄹ/을**:
- Add **~을** to stems ending in a consonant (**먹다** = **먹을**)
- Add **~ㄹ** directly to stems ending in a vowel (**가다** = **갈**)

Examples:
- **내가 갈 곳** = The place I will go
- **내가 먹을 음식** = The food I will eat
- **내가 가르칠 학생** = The student I will teach

More examples:
- **제가 갈 곳은 제주도예요** = The place I will go is Jeju-do
- **저는 할 일이 있어요** = I have something/work to do
- **제가 받을 점수는 중요해요** = The score I will receive is important

---

### So What's the Point?: ~는 것

If you ever want to change a sentence (or any part of a sentence) into a noun, you must do so by adding **~는 것** to the clause.

Example:
- **저는 사과를 원해요** = I want apples
- **저는 친구가 사과를 가져오는 것을 원해요** = I want my friend to bring apples

The structure is:
- I want (apples)
- I want (my friend to bring apples)

In Korean:
- **저는 (사과)를 원해요**
- **저는 (친구가 사과를 가져오는 것)을 원해요**

Another example:
- **저는 영화를 좋아해요** = I like movies
- **저는 영화를 만드는 것을 좋아해요** = I like making movies
- **저는 영화를 보는 것을 좋아해요** = I like watching movies

**~는 것** allows you to turn entire sentences into nouns so they can be used as objects, subjects, or other parts of a sentence.
