## Mimetic Verbs – 의태어 – Adding feeling, emphasis and imagery

Mimetic words attempt to mimic a particular motion or feeling to give the listener/reader more imagery. Onomatopoeias represent sound; mimetic words represent motion or feeling.

## Mimetic words with two syllables where one syllable is repeated

- **엉엉** = adverb for somebody crying (commonly used with 울다)
- **졸졸** = adverb for water coming out slowly (commonly used with 새다 or 흐르다)
- **뻘뻘** = adverb for somebody sweating (commonly used with 땀이 흘리다)
- **꼭꼭** = adverb for doing something repeatedly with might (commonly used with 씹다, 주무르다)
- **철철** = adverb for water, blood or energy flowing (commonly used with 피를 흐리다)
- **낄낄** = adverb for somebody snickering (commonly used with 웃다)
- **빙빙** = adverb for one going around something (commonly used with 돌다)
- **벅벅** = adverb for somebody scratching (commonly used with 긁다)
- **콕콕** = adverb for somebody having a needle-like pain (commonly used with 아프다)
- **펑펑** = adverb for using an item frivolously (commonly used with 쓰다)
- **꽁꽁** = adverb for something being frozen (commonly used with 얼다)
- **텅텅** = adverb for something being empty (commonly used with 비다)

## Adding ~거리다

~거리다 can be added to most mimetic words to turn them into a verb. The most common usage is to attach ~(으)면서 or ~(으)며 to the ~거리다 form, then place the commonly used verb after it.

- 그 영화가 너무 슬퍼서 보는 내내 **엉엉거리면서** 울었어요 = That movie was really sad so the entire time I was crying
- 저는 시냇물이 **졸졸거리며** 흐르는 소리를 듣는 것을 좋아해요 = I like listening to the sound of a stream flowing
- 주말 내내 **뻘뻘거리며** 돌아다녔더니 몸살이 났어요 = I was busily going around all weekend that now my body is sore
